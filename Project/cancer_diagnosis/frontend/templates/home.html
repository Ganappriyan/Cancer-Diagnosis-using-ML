Cancer Diagnosis using Machine Learning

This project aims to diagnose cervical cancer using machine learning algorithms. We have trained our models to detect Dyskeratotic, Koilocytic, Metaplastic, Parabasal, Superficial-Intermediate cells in cervical smear images, and classify them into one of four categories: High-grade squamous intraepithelial lesion (HSIL), Low-grade squamous intraepithelial lesion (LSIL), Negative for intraepithelial malignancy, and Squamous cell carcinoma.

Data Trained for:

Cell Types:
  • Dyskeratotic: Dyskeratotic cells are characterized by abnormal keratinization. They can be a sign of inflammation or damage to the epithelial tissue, and may be seen in certain types of cancer.
  • Koilocytic: Koilocytic cells are characterized by a clear halo around the nucleus. They are often caused by the human papillomavirus (HPV) and can be a sign of precancerous or cancerous changes.
  • Metaplastic: Metaplastic cells are normal cells that have undergone changes in response to injury or inflammation. They can be a sign of a benign or malignant process.
  • Parabasal: Parabasal cells are immature cells that are normally found in the basal layer of the epithelium. They can be a sign of inflammation, infection, or precancerous changes.
  • Superficial-Intermediate: Superficial-Intermediate cells are cells that have begun to differentiate and move towards the surface of the epithelium. They can be a sign of inflammation or infection, but are usually not associated with cancer.
Classification:
  • High-grade squamous intraepithelial lesion (HSIL): A precancerous condition where the cells in the cervical or vaginal epithelium are severely abnormal. It is often caused by the human papillomavirus (HPV) and can lead to cervical cancer if left untreated.
  • Low-grade squamous intraepithelial lesion (LSIL): A precancerous condition where the cells in the cervical or vaginal epithelium are mildly to moderately abnormal. It is often caused by the human papillomavirus (HPV) and may progress to HSIL if left untreated.
  • Negative for intraepithelial malignancy: No abnormal cells were detected in the sample.
  • Squamous cell carcinoma: A type of cancer that arises from the squamous cells in the epithelium. It can be aggressive and may spread to other parts of the body.
